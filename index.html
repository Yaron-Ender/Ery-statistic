Statistic Analysis For Erythromycin Assay Test

Written By : Yaron Ender, QC Laboratory

inroduction:

During the erythromycin manufacturing process, the substance is divided into six storage containers (bulk batches) originating from different locations. An assay determination test was performed on each batch according to Monograph QC.W.B.30301001, with an assay specification of 1.95–2.30. This report examines the possibility of reducing the number of assay tests by evaluating repeatability and variance using inferential statistics
All calculations were performed using Minitab software.

Bulk analysis

1. Cpk

\[ Cpk = \min \left( \frac{USL - \mu}{3\sigma},\; \frac{\mu - LSL}{3\sigma} \right) \]

The following table summarizes the Cpk values and standard deviations for each test sample:

ts 1 2 3 4 5 6
Cpk 1.11 1.23 1.11 1.52 1.24 1.78
stDev 0.048 0.063 0.055 0.043 0.044 0.03

AAs the table indicates, all test samples have Cpk values well above the industry-accepted threshold of 1.0, demonstrating that our process is capable of producing results within acceptable limits. Notably, test samples 4 and 6 exceed the benchmark of 1.33, indicating exceptional process capability.

2.Confidence interval

\[ \bar{x} \pm t_{\frac{\alpha}{2}} \times \frac{S}{\sqrt{n}} \]

All six test samples (ts1 through ts6) have 99% confidence intervals for the mean that lie comfortably within the assay specification range of 1.95 – 2.30 .

3. ANOVA one-way test

A one-way ANOVA was conducted at a 99% confidence level on the six mean replicates to determine whether there were significant differences among them. The hypotheses were defined as follows:

Null Hypothesis: Alternative Hypothesis:
There is no difference between the six test samples with respect to assay measurement. There is a difference between the six test samples with respect to assay measurement.

The ANOVA results indicated no statistically significant difference among the test samples
(p > 0.01). Therefore, we fail to reject the null hypothesis.

4. Coefficient of Variation (RSD)

\[ CV = RSD =\frac{\sigma}{\mu}\times\;100\% \]

A homogeneity analysis was performed within each batch.

The results show that all bulk batches have an RSD of less than 5%, with 85% of them having an RSD ≤ 1%. This very low RSD indicates excellent homogeneity within the batches.

Conclusion

The statistical analysis clearly demonstrates that the erythromycin assay process is both robust and consistent. All test samples yielded Cpk values well above the minimum requirement—with several samples even surpassing more stringent benchmarks—indicating a highly capable process. The 99% confidence intervals for assay measurements consistently fell within the specified range, and the one-way ANOVA confirmed that no significant differences exist among the replicates. Furthermore, the extremely low coefficient of variation (RSD) underscores the excellent homogeneity of the bulk batches.
Therefore, the analysis supports reducing assay testing in the bulk batches.
